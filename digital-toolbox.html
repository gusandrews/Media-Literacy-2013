Course Description


The Digital Toolbox provides essential skills in digital media applications including developing text, still and moving images, information graphics, and audio files for Web-based presentation for a wide range of communication professions and serves as a foundation for more advanced courses involving Web-based production. Offered Fall and Spring.

Introduction to the Course


This course addresses technological convergence and the reality that all students must have an introductory knowledge of digital production. The Digital Toolbox focuses on the ways in which digital communication tools can be used to support the types of narratives that communication students are likely to produce (news, magazines, press releases, fictional narratives for small screens, and so on). It is a designed as a lab-based course, allowing students to develop a set of basic skills required for the manipulation of sound and image in digital environments. Students will learn the basics of Illustrator (graphic design software), Photoshop (photo editing software), InDesign (print and digital publishing software), Final Cut Pro (video editing software), and Wordpress (web content management system). For students of communication, these skills are essential to basic literacy; without them, they have little hope of success in their chosen areas of expertise.

In addition, this course will help to foster media literacy, a primary goal of the Communication and Media Arts departments. Although this is primarily a “how to” course, the knowledge of what is behind the production of digital narratives gives students a glimpse of how editorial and creative decisions shape digital content. In challenging students to reflect on their own decisions, this course will support our effort to create both ethical producers and informed consumers of digital content, in whatever form of delivery. Students in all Communication concentrations – Advertising, Communication Studies, Interactive Media, Journalism, Public Relations/Organizational Communication, Radio, TV & Film, and Sports Communication – need the technological tools associated with digital content for their upper-level courses, internships and jobs.

Course Objectives


This class will introduce students to the basic principles and techniques of digital media. Students will develop a set of basic skills required for the manipulation of sound and image in digital environments. In addition, students will learn the basics of Illustrator (graphic design software), Photoshop (photo editing software), InDesign (print and digital publishing software), Final Cut Pro (video editing software), and Wordpress.com (web content management system).

Students will critique their own and each other’s work in class with the intent to give helpful feedback. There will be many opportunities for improvement. The premise is that one learns more effectively through extensive practice and revising. In addition to lectures and class discussions, small group work and class exercises will be utilized. Specific objectives for students are:
  1. Understand the role of digital media in a wide range of communication & media arts fields. (Projects)
  2. Have an essential vocabulary relevant to digital media applications. (Projects)
  3. Be able to create still and moving visual content for the Web. (Projects)
  4. Have a basic understanding of how to use content management systems. (Projects, website)
  5. Be able to create a digital portfolio integrating all of the skills listed above. (Website)

Achievement of these outcomes will be assessed through multiple projects and a final presentation.

Assignments & Evaluation Policies: aka How to Do Well in This Class

To help you develop basic skills, you will complete projects with digital tools used in web development, publishing, advertising, and professional video editing. These are a Magazine Cover, Infographic, Portfolio Animation, and Ad Parody. For the final project, all of these will be posted on your culminating Project Website. Think of this website as part of your resumé -- it can be the beginning of a professional portfolio that you can show to employers to demonstrate that you know your stuff when it comes to the software we're using. (Of course This Is Not The Last Image/Video/Website You'll Work On™, so don't stress if you're a perfectionist. Just do the best you can for this course. After the course is over you can totally take it down/keep improving it/replace poor-quality projects with better examples of your work/etc.)

Classwork will consist of:

  • Brief introductions to some basics of the software
  • Sharing skills with your peers
  • Working on projects
  • Workshopping your work.

Class participation and attendance are required. ESPECIALLY on workshop days. You will be expected to contribute positive evaluations of your classmates' work (what "works" about the piece for you?), suggestions for other approaches to working with software or aesthetically evaluating a piece ("Have you tried...?" "I had the same problem and here's a trick that worked for me"), and constructive critical evaluations of what could be improved ("I wanted to see more of..." "Did you notice...?")

During class lab time, you need to be "on task" working on some element of the project -- not randomly surfing the Web, chatting with people in or out of class about stuff other than the assignment, working on other assignments, etc. If you think you've already finished the basics of an assignment, talk to me about what else you can do to improve it. You can also teach yourself a more advanced skill with the software, document that skill on the class site, and/or help your peers with the basics.

Homework will consist of:

  • Finding a tutorial
    • Sharing the tutorial you found with the class,
    • Working through two tutorials yourself -- the one you found, and one shared by someone else in class
  • Posting a question about something you didn't understand on iLearn OR
  • Posting tips and tricks about something you learned on iLearn.

It may seem easy to ignore the tutorial homework, but be aware: I may call on you in class to demonstrate something from the tutorial you posted or from the other tutorial you tried out. This will count towards your grade.

To hand in your assignments, send them via the Marist Dropbox service. Do not email them to me. You'll find that many of the files are big enough to cause problems with email, anyway -- they might bounce, meaning I won't get them, and then you'll be up a creek.

Assignments must be turned in in two formats:

  1. the format native to the program we're using (Photoshop .psd, Illustrator, InDesign, Final Cut Pro, and raw HTML/CSS)
  2. a production image (.jpg or .png for image files, .mp4 or .mov for movies, a posted working website for the final project.
This makes it so I can see your work -- what you know about layers, how you have put together your cuts, etc. This will be considered in your grade. Not turning in the native format file may raise red flags of cheating, and I may begin investigating whether you have copied your work.

All assignments are due at noon on the due date. Work turned in to the Drop Box after this time will result in 5 points per day being deducted from the earned grade on that project.

GRADING

Here's how your grade will break down:

  • Photoshop magazine cover - 20%
  • Illustrator infographic - 20%
  • Final Cut ad parody, including script, storyboard, and the movie itself - 30%
  • Web portfolio, including HTML, CSS, and InDesign - 10%
  • Class participation including posting and commenting on the wiki for homework, your participation in workshop critique, how much you challenge yourself, and how much progress you personally make from what you initially knew how to do when you started the class - 20%
Here's what the grades will represent:
  • A. An A student is one who, in addition to all the qualities manifested by a B student, seeks mastery of a special field; s/he has initiative and originality in attacking and solving problems; s/he shows ability in rethinking problems, making associations, and adapting to new and changing situations; moreover, s/ he has an appropriate vocabulary at his or her command.
  • B. To earn a B grade a student must manifest all the qualities characteristic of a C student and in addition be able to demonstrate skills beyond the basic elements of the course; s/he has a more personal grasp of the principles of the course and perceives wider application of them. The student should be able to discuss the subject matter of the course with ease.
  • C. To earn a C grade a student must be able to demonstrate the basic skills of a course, understand the essential background and materials of a course, apply the basic principles involved, and express them intelligibly.
  • D. A student who is deficient in some degree in any of the areas that are characteristic of a C grade will earn a D.
  • F. The student has failed to show mastery of the basic subject matter for the course.

The grades of A-, B+, B-, C+, C-, D+, D- are used to indicate that a student has shown more or less than the usual competency required for that grade.

Any work that receives a grade of less than a C may revised within two weeks of the student receiving her/his grade. If a work receives a grade of less than a C because it was originally turned in after its due date (it was late), it may not be revised.

Cheating of any kind in this class will result in you being dropped from the class, a grade of “F” recorded as your final grade, and your name forwarded to the Dean and to the Center for Advising and Academic Services. Helping each other understand what to do and how to do it is not cheating. Getting advice is not cheating. Doing someone else's work for them, borrowing someone else's work, or having work done for you is always cheating, in this class and pretty much everywhere else in the world.
